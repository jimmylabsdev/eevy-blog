Back When Planning Was Part of Driving

It took a little more than 50 years after independence for the first ‘petrol bunk’ to open up at our village. Until then, every time my uncle hired a car to go somewhere, there was some serious amount of planning that used to go in before the trip - which route to take, where to eat, where to spend the night, and most importantly, where to refuel.

I think I picked up on that habit when I bought my first car - the WagonR. It used to also run on LPG, for which I had actually paid extra. But in those days (around 2005), LPG refill stations were not so commonly available in Bangalore. I used to keep my Sundays to drive down to a mall to fill up on groceries and also on LPG while on the way back. I’m sure this is the case even today for LPG and CNG vehicles.

We are all great planners by default, essentially because our options are limited and it’s easy to feel lost if unplanned - The Anxiety.

So, what is range anxiety really and why is it being pulled up in all EV discussions?

What Exactly Is Range Anxiety?

Let’s go back to my earlier example. I remember doing a solo trip to Murudeswar - on the Western coast, very close to Gokarna and Goa - in my WagonR (LPG). The route was through Shimoga and had a lot of ghat sections. This was going to be an unplanned trip - I needed a break from work, badly - all I had done was filled up on LPG the previous day evening. I checked Google Maps and this was the only recommended route. No other planning - where to have breakfast, where to stay, where to refuel - nothing. I just left leaving everything to fate. I reached Shimoga by afternoon and after a quick lunch, I was on my way towards Honnavara. I found an LPG refuelling bunk, a proper petrol bunk adjacent, at the outskirts of Shimoga, all by mere luck. Feeling extremely happy after filling up on LPG I went on.

When The Fuel Gauge Started Dropping

By the time I reached Honnavara, the LPG meter was dwindling again. I assumed it was because of all the ghat-section I just navigated through and so panic set in. I was 100% sure I will reach the destination, yet I was still worried.

“What if there is some kind of road block?” “What if there is a traffic jam?” “What if I get lost on the way and take a longer route?” Most importantly - “How will i drive back?”

I inquired with some folks when I stopped for tea and no one knew if there was an LPG station anywhere nearby. I was told to go to Mangalore or Margaon and try.

The Backup Plan Saved Me

But my car had another trick up it’s sleeve - I just did not ‘want’ to use it because it simply defeats the entire purpose of paying extra for an LPG vehicle. While it had a puny little LPG tank sitting where the spare wheel would have otherwise sat, it also had a regular petrol tank.

Petrol used to be way cheaper back then compared to the current price, but I digress.

My issue was, I paid extra for the factory-fitted LPG kit, so I can run the car on an even cheaper fuel - LPG and I hated the thought of running the car on petrol for that same reason.

Anyways, getting back to my story - Even though I knew that the anxiety was only superficial and purely in my mind, I was worried nevertheless. Fact is, I eventually had to give in and ‘splurge’ a little on petrol and made it back to Bangalore after a couple of days. And I eventually stopped and refuelled LPG back when I reached Shimoga, to restore some sanity.

Range Anxiety Is Mostly Fear Of Uncertainty

Now, imagine if I were driving on the same route in an electric vehicle.

I honestly do not know how well-equipped the stretch of road is right now with the charging infrastructure. But if the situation was anything similar, and considering no alternate fuel coming to my rescue as it did last time - I cannot imagine how anxious I would be right from the start.

And that’s the thing about range anxiety — it isn’t always about actually running out of charge. Sometimes, it’s simply the fear of uncertainty. We Indians have lived with that anxiety for decades with LPG, CNG and even petrol vehicles in remote regions. EVs are simply the latest technology forcing us to confront it again in a slightly different era.

Planning Still Matters — Just Differently

But with the amount of technology at our disposal today, even doing 20% of the planning we used to do earlier can significantly reduce that anxiety.

I know -

  • The charging infrastructure still isn’t strong enough.
  • The promised range figures are not always entirely accurate.
  • Real-world range depends heavily on how and where you drive. etc., etc.

Simple things like -

  • Identifying reliable charging stations on the route.
  • Having backup charging options in case the primary one is occupied or inoperable.
  • Knowing whether your destination has charging facilities available.

Help a lot.

And for people like me, range anxiety probably comes naturally, after all I feel anxious driving my petrol vehicle on highways even today.

But with a little bit of planning the ordeal can be minimized to a larger extent and the drive enjoyable again.
