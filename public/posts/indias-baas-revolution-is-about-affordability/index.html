The New Urban Cruiser Ebella -

Toyota offically launched its first battery-electric vehicle for the Indian market, on May 29th named - the Ebella. It is expected to be available in 3 different variants - Only the top-spec E3 variant is priced at ₹23.60 lakh (ex-showroom) was announced yesterday. Even the official website only shows the specs for the E3. I’m assuming there will be E2 and E1 naturally which will be revealed in the near-future. However, the website and the brochure show a BaaS version at ₹15.25 Lakhs + a Bettery EMI of ₹4.99 per Km. It isn’t very clear from the details if E3 is also being offered in the BaaS format or if BaaS is a variant on its own. The Ebella also comes with a 60% assured buy back and an eight year battery warranty. You can look up other details on their website or go through the number of YouTube videos around this car to learn more.

And so - no, this is not a paid review. We are focussing on this launch for a very different reason - BaaS. What is BaaS really? Up until recently (read - until 2 months ago), I used to think that BaaS meant - battery replacement or battery swapping. Please don’t laugh because there are countries like China and some other South East Asian countries that have this facility.

But In India BaaS means something entirely different.

Before dwelling into the details, lets look at one more example just to increase the sample size. Since the Ebella is in reality a rebranded or rebadged e vitara, lets also take a look at what they offer.

The Maruti Suzuki e Vitara was officially launched in India on February 17, 2026 and was also Maruti’s first-ever electric SUV. The BaaS version for the e vitara starts from ₹10.99 lakh + ₹3.99 per km.

Given the close relationship between the two vehicles and the identical claimed range figures, it appears they share significant portions of their underlying EV architecture.

From what I understand, the BaaS offered for both vehicles are actually a way to get the vehicle for a cheaper price and pay the cost of the battery - which is not essentially sold as a separate part - in monthly EMIs.

Here’s my observations about this idea -

It’s essentially a pricing strategy to reduce the sticker shock but I doubt if it really reduces the total ownership cost in anyway. I agree that pricing is probably a huge hurdle that’s come in the way of us transitioning from ICE to EVs. So, I feel with the BaaS model, OEMs seem to be solving the purchase decision. But on hindsight they might be introducing new ownership complexities that buyers should understand.

Let me tell you why -

The Subscription Contradiction-

I hope you’ll agree that almost all Indian cars are already commonly financed through loans and that only a handful of cars are bought using full down-payment.

So add ‘BaaS’ to the picture and you end up paying two EMIs for what’s essentially the same car.

Now here’s the kicker - Everytime you charge-up your car using either the home charger setup or a public charger, you pay ₹X for a % of battery charge, which translates to the number of kms you can drive until the next charge. Now, for the same amount of kms that you drive between each charge your battery subscription payment also keeps accumulating - A Double Whammy!

Ofcourse, in today’s world we do end up paying for subscriptions in one form or another. We all pay multiple subscriptions for OTT platforms - Netflix, Jio, Prime, etc. And then there are subscriptions for books, subscriptions for music, subscriptions for food, etc., etc. But all these subscriptions give you something tangible - something that can, at some level, make your brain secrete some dopamine and endorphines - the pleasure hormones.

On the other hand, the monthly battery subscription (or BaaS) itself doesn’t provide anything so rewarding, not even some extra charge. All it provides is the right to use the battery - that powers the car that you already are paid for or continue to pay a monthly EMI for.

The Financing Side of the Equation

Now, let’s look at some practical questions that arise from the BaaS by navigating to the other side of the equation - The Financiers. Similar to how you get your car or your house financed by a bank, the battery in a BaaS purchase is also financed by a suitable financier and I assume some form of security interest or ownership arrangement exists between the financier and the battery, though the exact structure is unclear to me.

We all know that for a financed car loan, even if you miss a single EMI, the collection recovery sevice is triggered. You are bombarded by multiple calls reminding your to make your payments, and persistent non-payment can eventually trigger recovery proceedings.

Question is - what happens if you miss a BaaS payment? Is the recovery service triggered and the battery alone recovered in extreme cases and the car rendered unusable? Or like other subscription service - is your subscription put ‘on-hold’ somehow - car locked remotely or battery charging locked out remotely?

It’s not about - “Can a repossessed battery make the car unusable?”, but “Can the battery itself be repossessed in the first place, independently?”

How is the insurance handled for vehicles purchased under BaaS subscription? Is the battery insured separately from the vehicle since it belongs to a different financer? Which financer gets the cover amount incase of an untoward accident and how is the money split?

While Toyota offers an assured buy-back, similar to how MG offers for the Windsor - one thing is not clear - how is the second owner given full rights to both the vehicle and the battery?

The legality and ownership structure of these vehicles bought in BaaS mode remains unclear to me, particularly in scenarios involving defaults, resale, insurance claims and battery ownership disputes.

A Lot of Questions To Be Answered

Given the number of unanswered questions around ownership, insurance and recovery, I would like to understand the legal framework governing BaaS arrangements better.

The truth is - more I researched BaaS, the less it looked like an EV technology innovation and the more it looked like a financing innovation. The question is whether financing innovation is really the bottleneck preventing EV adoption in India, or whether the industry is solving the wrong problem.

Maybe its time I do some grunt work, talk to the Toyota, MG, Maruti showrooms to get some clarity on things. Maybe even talk to some Insurance companies like ‘Acko’ and some banks about all this to get some more clarity.

To be clear, I’m not arguing that BaaS is a bad idea. I’m arguing that I still don’t fully understand how it works in practice, and the answers are surprisingly difficult to find despite multiple OEMs now pushing the model aggressively.
