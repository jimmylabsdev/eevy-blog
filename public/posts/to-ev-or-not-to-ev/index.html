A Thought That Started It All

Imagine this - The petrol car that you own is 3 years old, run for about 30k kms and recently completed its third free service for which you ended up paying ₹12k, and feel cheated. It is an underwhelming car, nothing really exciting in terms of performance, mileage, nothing. Life’s become noring and costly with this car.

That is my story right now.

I bought a petrol car back in 2023, after returning from Europe. I did not do proper research and out of desperation and pressure bought a boring little 7 seater. Life has been slow ever since I drove the car out the showroom.

Today (exactly 3 years since I bought the car), while I was renewing my insurance, I thought to myself -
“Hey, why not go for a change.”

My Car Journey

Honestly, this is my 4th four-wheeler and I have usually been very proud of what I drove - Wagon R (tall stance), Punto (fantastic 1.3L engine), XUV500 (because it’s XUV500) and I finally ended up with my latest petrol car.

The Big Question

So here I am thinking, if I hate it so much then maybe it is time for a change.

But then to what?

Unlike in 2023, today I have so many options - Bolero neo, Scorpio N, Seltos, etc., etc., to name a few. These are my standard go-to options, that have been around long enough (few more than others) that everybody else goes to too.

And then there are EVs - new breed of cars taking over the road from their grand-old counterparts.

Are EVs the Obvious Choice?

The recent geo-political developments have reshaped how we really think about EVs. Afterall if you switched to induction stoves, why not to EVs. Right?

Not necessarily.

There are so many other factors at play here. It’s not like you can simply plug into a socket and start driving - actually you can, just that it is not so straight forward. Isn’t it?

The Bigger Reality

Let’s be honest, we import almost 90% of all our crude oil to meet our domestic demands. Without going too much into the details, we already know how much of a headache importing crude oil has been over the years and especially recently due to the geopolitical developments.

And then there’s the environmental impact - if we are the second largest importer, we might also be the burning so much - obviously, right? We’ve seen this every year during the winters for more than a decade. More and more of our cities get added to thsi list every year and this situation is also getting worse by the day.

Overall, crude oil has been on shaky grounds recently.

Should We Switch?

So, should we just cross-over to the other side?

The answer is NO.

Because the other side is still, not only in India but even globally.

So what’s really the situation - are we there yet?

What Would It Take?

First and foremost - there is a huge shift in mindset that’s required before moving to EVs.

Today, my car buying decision is primarily based on my budget - Im a family man with two kids in a city like Bangalore - budget is always the deciding factor for all instance and purposes. I walk into a Maruti showroom and pick the car that best suits my budget and spend a little more here and there to “add-on” whatever comfort add-ons I want.

Buying an EV is a little more complex than that.

A. Range

First comes the usage - I drive 40kms daily during weekdays. Then there are trips to my home town - a mere 380 kms away - during weekends and holidays. And then there are occasionaly really long trips like Goa, Murdeswar, etc., once a year.

Do you see where I am going with this?

I can’t just walk in to a showroom and buy what my budget allows but I have to buy what gives me the ‘range’ I need to cover these distances without any major hiccups.

B. Charging

Now, with a petrol or diesel car, I can easily do the trips I mentioned earlier, with refilling stations available almost everywhere on every highway nowadays.

But with an EV, it needs a lot of planning as you can imagine.

And not to forget the types of charging opions available.

From what I’ve read and understood so far:

  • Type 2 → AC slow charging
  • CCS2 → Fast charging (~25 mins to a few hours)

C. Budget

You usage decides your battery capacity and hence the range you need your on your EV. This inturn controls the cost of the car.

The higher the range, the higher the cost.

Battery alone contributes:

  • ~25% to 40% of vehicle cost

Plus:

  • Thermal management
  • Weight distribution
  • Software systems

So, Are EVs Expensive?

Simply put - yes.

But only if you consider upfront cost.

The Math (This Is Where It Gets Interesting)

Let’s say:

  • Mileage: ~10–12 kmpl
  • Monthly driving: ~1250 km

The Realization

High upfront cost
→ Extremely low running cost

Final Take

  • Yes, EVs are costly to buy
  • Yes, they are cheaper to run and maintain
  • Yes, infrastructure is still catching up
  • Yes, EVs are here to stay

Closing Thought

And, it looks like EVs are here to stay and maybe I should start considering this segment more seriously, when researching for new cars for myself and my family.

And if I am unable to afford one now (and it could be true too), it is probably time to consider it as a worthy contender to learn and research more about it.

So stay with me, by following me here and together we can explore, learn and eventually buy ourselves that perfect car.
