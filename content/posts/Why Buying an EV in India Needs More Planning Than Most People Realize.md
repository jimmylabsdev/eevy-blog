## The Test Drive Is Probably The Last Thing You Need

Last night, while trying to research on the new e Vitara and Maruti's Battery-as-a-Service (BaaS) offer, I stumbled upon the callback form and filled it up.

Today morning, promptly I received the call from the nearest Nexa showroom.

Like any regular buyer, I told them that I wanted information about the e Vitara and the BaaS offer. The caller tried explaining the details, but when I started probing a little deeper, she suggested that her manager would be able to answer my questions better and promised a callback in 10 minutes.

That 10 minutes never came.

Instead, I received another call from their customer service team asking if my questions had been answered and whether I had been offered a test drive.

And that got me thinking.

Maybe the manager forgetting to call back was a one-off incident. People get busy. Things fall through the cracks.

What surprised me wasn't the missed callback. It was the fact that the conversation had already moved towards a test drive. 

*No, I wasn't offered a test drive because I never asked for one in the first place. I wasn't calling about the petrol Vitara. I wanted to understand the e Vitara. A test drive isn't going to help me much until I understand the basics first.*

It's like a well rehearsed, synchronised routine - Buyers jump straight to checking out the vehicle, and sellers jump straight to offering a test drive. But this routine might work surprisingly well for a traditional car, but for an EV ... ?

The more I thought about it, the more I realised that this is probably where a lot of EV conversations tend to go wrong.

Like me, most people who walk into a showroom today to look at an EV are transitioning from a petrol or diesel vehicle. Their questions are different.

*Can I charge at home? How much will charging cost me? What happens if I live in an apartment? What happens to the battery after 8 years? What is BaaS and does it actually save me money? How does the insurance work?*

And maybe that's why I keep repeating the same thing - Buying an EV is not like buying that petrol car. Though EVs are a lot of fun, there is a certain amount of planning that is required to be able to enjoy a seamless ownership.

## Can You Actually Charge An EV Conveniently?

That is one of the very basic questions that you need to answer before making any purchase decision - Can you charge an EV at home or not?

Yes, you can charge the EV at your office parking, if you are lucky enough.

Yes, your neighbourhood probably has a public charging spot situated very close to your place of residence.

But these cannot be the deciding factors. 

Public chargers are useful for top-ups, emergencies and long-distance travel. But for most buyers, convenient home charging remains the most economical option.

So definitely, you need a home charger and while most OEMs readily provide one for you, you still need to understand if you need permission from your landlord, association, and the electricity board. There are additional costs for the wiring, conduit and civil work that you need to prepare for.

These are the basic requirements that you need to prepare for in advance - not a Test Drive. The dealer might not necessarily probe you on these, but without these questions cleared, you should not even walk into a showroom.

## The Economics Need To Work For You

When I bought the XUV500 way back in 2017, they told me that maintenance or service cost will be around ₹4000 every 10k kms - unbelievable but I loved it. When I bought my current car, I was told the same story but that never came true. So, how does it work for an EV?

You need to first consider the charging cost - something that I already covered in one of my previous articles - how much it costs at a highway fast charger vs what it costs at a home charger.

Then comes the maintenance cost - since it does not have a traditional combustion motor, it requires less oils to keep things in good condition. But an EV does need regular maintenance nevertheless.

Another thing to consider is the financing and insurance options available for EVs. 
While EV financing and insurance are available now, buyers should understand how batteries are treated, especially in BaaS models where the vehicle and battery may be financed separately.

And so an equally important question is - can you finance and insure the EV independently using options that do not already have a tieup with the dealer.

## Understanding your usecase of battery

*Your daily need decides the overall cost of the vehicle not the nice to have features.*

For example, I use my car mostly for city drives - pickup and drop for my kids, to and from school, weekend shopping at the mall, visiting friends and occasionally to drive down to my village near Namakkal in TN. Apart from this there is also long vacation-type trips once or twice a year, which my family like to do. That's my overall foreseeable usage. 

Since I have a mix of city and highway driving requirement (and I do not intend to own two cars for each purpose), I need a car that will have a considerable mileage per charge. So may be if I am look at an e-vitara, I may want decide on the higher end 61 kWH battery pack rather than the lower end 49 kWh one.

For someone who lives locally and needs a vehicle only for city driving with rare or short highway driving - he or she may settle with a 49kWh battery.

Why is it important? Because the type of battery you choose decides the cost of your vehicle.

So unlike petrol or diesel cars where features and convenience dictate the cost, usability and range dictate the buying cost of your EV.

## Your location also matters

We've covered this space more or less earlier, but here's what I haven't talked about.

Your place of residence really matters and is also a deciding factor for your purchase -

For me, since I stay in Bangalore, purchasing an EV with sensible amount range makes a lot of sense. Now imagine if I permanently stayed at my village - would I consider buying an EV. For me personally, the answer is probably 'NO' and here's why - 

The current infrastructure in and around my village cannot support an EV. The nearest public charging station is nearly 42 kms from my village (~ 1 hour drive). 

Finding a mechanic or tow vehicle incase of a breakdown is another challenge due to the village setting and since you have to depend heavily on the dealer or OEM service network for even a quick fix.

## Buy The Ecosystem First. The Car Second.

Its like a marriage - When buying an EV - You don't just buy a car, you buy the entire ecosystem that supports it too.

And I believe that's probably the biggest mistake many of us make when evaluating an EV - We are never prepared enough.

We walk into the showroom and immediately start evaluating the vehicle.
The range. The features. The touchscreen. The ADAS package. The acceleration.

But the vehicle is only one part of the equation.
What about the ecosystem without which the EV cannot exist?

- *Can you charge at home?*
- *Does your locality have the necessary charging infrastructure?*
- *Does the ownership cost make sense for your driving pattern?*
- *Have you chosen the right battery size for your actual usage?*
- *What are my financing options? What's BaaS? Is it also being offered?*

These are questions that need answers before you even begin comparing features and taking test drives.

Maybe that's why my conversation with the showroom felt incomplete - The test drive was being offered before the fundamentals were cleared.

And perhaps that's where many EV buying journeys start going wrong.

If you're considering an EV, my suggestion is simple:

***Consider the ecosystem first.*
***Buy the car second.*

Because once the ecosystem works for your lifestyle, choosing the vehicle becomes the easy part.
