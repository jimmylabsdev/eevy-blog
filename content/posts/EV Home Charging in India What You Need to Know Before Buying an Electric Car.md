### Understanding the Type, Requirements and the Installation Basics

If I continue on this path of ultimately buying an EV for my daily use, one of the critical criteria today would be home charging. And since I live in a major city like Bangalore, and in an independent house, that decision becomes very easy. I have a 3 phase connection at home, the OEM will provide for a home charging unit and will also, most of the times, arrange for the installation.

But what do I do when we drive down to our village, what do I do regarding overnight charging? I cannot just depend on a public charging setup - which is already an hour's drive away from our farm.

Home charging is more than just a nice to have facility. For people who own an EV, it is always better than depending on public charger.

### Type - Basic 15A unit

I am sure that many of is in India, will have a similar situation where one may be living in the city and would want to go to their village - or a second home frequently.

Fact is, there are multiple options at hand for me - and that too without much of a fuss.

Almost all EVs come with a portable-charger which can be plugged into a 15A power socket - the same sized socket that powers your fridge and washing machine.

So that alone can take care of my overnight charging requirement as long as my connect is able to reach the socket.

However, just to be safe I'd probably have an electrician draw a fresh new line with a new MCB and breaker setup (for added safety) and install a new socket much nearer to where I park.

The portable charger usually comes with a 3 pin plug that goes into the wall socket. The other end is connect to a charger box that contains the safety electronics and charging controls, which then connects to the car. The car manages the AC charging process and converts the incoming AC power into the DC power required by the battery.

### Type - Wall Mounted Options

These are more permanent setups that look more sturdy and less like a *'jugaad'*.

Some OEMs also provide this unit (as extra) and also provide for installation. I can purchase additional units, since I need one for my farm and have a local electrician install it for me or use someone recommended by the OEM.

These are usually wall mounted and are available in more than 2 types - 

- 3.3 kW
- 7.4 kW
- 11 kW
- 22 kW and so on

Most EVs sold in India can be charged using a 3.3 kW AC charger. A 7.4 kW unit can charge faster, provided your car is compatible. But these the most commonly used charging units.

Some 3.3 kW units come with a 3-pin socket that can connect with the portable charger, while some come with a dedicated Type 2 connector that can connect directly with the car.

The 7.4 kW unit also usually comes with a Type 2 that can be connected to the car directly.

The easiest and the go-to setup for someone like me - who needs an additional charger at my second home in the village - is to setup a simple 15A + MCB + Breaker setup. I will need to have a strong earthing arrangement, which I think exists already, but I will have it checked by an electrician nevertheless.

A more dedicated setup would be the 3.3 kW charger, which I could procure from Bangalore itself and get it installed at my village or see if there is any local delear much closer to my village.

Factory-manufactured charging units typically include additional safety and weather-protection features. They also provide a cleaner and more permanent installation compared to an improvised charging setup.

Depending on the charger type and the location of the installation, you may need:

- New wiring
- Dedicated protection devices
- Longer cable runs and additional conduits
- Additional electrical & civil work
- A rock-solid earthing setup

### Would I install the 7.4 kW unit or higher?

Maybe, if my car is compatible and if I need a quicker charge. But I would definitely look at the nuances and understand this category of chargers.

1. The higher the power, does not necessarily mean quicker charge.
2. Higher powerd charger may need a different setup, where you may have to get the assigned load increased by your ESCOM.
3. Your EV should also support the spped. A 7.4 kW charger won't charge faster if the vehicle's onboard charger is limited to 3.3 kW. 

*Remember, charging speed is only as fast as the slowest component in the charging chain. The limitation could be your home's electrical supply, the charger, or the car itself. Before investing in a higher-powered charger, check your vehicle's specifications to see the maximum charging speed it can actually support.*

But in general, very high powered charger may not really be required if you have a moderate usage and can allow the car to charge overnight without interruption.

After looking at the options available, I think most buyers should not obsess over charging speeds and charger specifications. The more important questions are:

- Do I have a dedicated parking space?
- Can I install a charger where I park?
- Does my home's electrical connection support the charger I want?
- Can I charge overnight consistently?

For most EV owners, a simple and reliable home charging setup is likely to be far more valuable than the fastest charger available.

Home charging is not really about charging speed. It is about convenience, predictability and starting every morning with enough charge for the day's drive.
