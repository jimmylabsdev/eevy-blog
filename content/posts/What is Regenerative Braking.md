## Free current anyone?

We were in Delhi at that time, and I was 11 when dad bought me and my brother our first cycles.

Mine was a simple kids’ cycle similar to my brother’s, but his was a little smaller in size (obviously) and a little more colorful too. So obviously, his looked a little jazzier than mine.

Dad had spent a little more so my brother would like the colours and grow an interest in riding, and to compensate, he got mine a headlight.

---

## The Dynamo Moment

The headlight was powered using a small dynamo attached to the back wheel.

I was only in grade 5, and electromagnetism was a little “out-of-syllabus” for my age. So all I could understand from the instructions the dealer gave me was that I had to push the dynamo a little so it came in contact with the wheel, and that would generate the “current” needed to power the headlight.

---

## First Fascination with Electricity

Yes, it was a fascinating thing to have, and no, I did not obsess over it asking questions like - can I light up our house with this, etc.

That’s more of a Gen Z or later kind of question. Back in the eighties and nineties, it was just as good as owning a flagship phone - you simply had the privilege to show off with it.

It was only later during my childhood that I learnt how dynamos work, but that early experience really helped me relate and understand.

---

## The Same Principle, Bigger Scale

Fact is, even today, electricity is more or less generated using the same principles, only at a much larger scale - unless, of course, you use solar panels.

Our cars and bikes today use a similar setup to power electrical systems - just that it is far more efficient and involves a battery.

---

## Starting with ICE Cars

So, let’s start from ICE cars (traditional petrol and diesel vehicles), and then we’ll wind our way to today’s EVs.

The petrol car that sits in your garage has something called an alternator - a fancier and more efficient version of a dynamo.

This alternator powers:

- Headlights  
- AC  
- Stereo  
- Other electrical systems  

It is also connected to a 12V battery, which stores and supplies energy, especially when idling or when the engine is off.

---

## Enter Hybrids

Then came hybrid vehicles, which brought in the concept of “regenerative braking,” at least to my knowledge.

Hybrids are a different breed:

- Not pure ICE  
- Not fully electric  
- Something in between  

These cars use traditional IC engines for propulsion, but with regenerative braking, they also recover energy to charge a separate battery.

This allows the vehicle to switch to electric power under certain conditions.

---

## EVs Take It Further

The same concept carries over to EVs as well.

Since these vehicles run purely on electricity and depend heavily on the battery, engineers figured:

**Why not recover some of the energy that would otherwise be lost?**

---

## How Regenerative Braking Works

The idea works like this:

- During acceleration → motor draws power from the battery  
- During deceleration → motor works in reverse as a generator  

This generated energy is fed back into the battery.

While each OEM may offer:

- Low  
- Medium  
- High settings  

The underlying principle remains the same.

---

## How It Differs from Regular Cars

Traditional petrol and diesel cars:

- Use an alternator connected to the engine  
- Generating electricity consumes engine power  

EVs:

- Use the drive motor itself as a generator  
- Recover energy during deceleration  

Instead of losing energy as heat in brakes, EVs reclaim part of it.

---

## Is It Really Efficient?

It depends on driving conditions.

- **City driving (stop-and-go)** → Highly effective  
- **Highway driving (constant speed)** → Less effective  

Regenerative braking kicks in the moment you release the accelerator and provides a gentle slowing effect.

---

## The Simple Truth

Regenerative braking isn’t some magical new tech.

It’s the same old idea of generating electricity from motion - just applied smarter.

Instead of wasting energy as heat:

→ EVs recover it  
→ Store it back in the battery  

---

## Why It Matters

Regenerative braking gives you a small but useful energy recovery.

It can help extend your range - sometimes just enough to reach the next charging point.

Much like the reserve fuel concept in traditional bikes.
