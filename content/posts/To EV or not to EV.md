## A Thought That Started It All

Imagine this - The petrol car that you own is 3 years old, run for about 30k kms and recently completed its third free service for which you ended up paying ₹12k, and feel cheated. It is an underwhelming car, nothing really exciting in terms of performance, mileage, nothing. Life's become boring and costly with this car.

That is my story right now. I bought a petrol car back in 2023, after returning from Europe. I did not do proper research and out of desperation and pressure bought a boring little 7 seater. Life has been slow ever since I drove the car out the showroom. Today (exactly 3 years since I bought the car), while I was renewing my insurance, I thought to myself - "Hey, why not go for a change."

## My Car Journey

Honestly, this is my 4th four-wheeler and I have usually been very proud of what I drove - Wagon R (tall stance), Punto (fantastic 1.3L engine), XUV500 (because it's XUV500) and I finally ended up with my latest petrol car.

So here I am thinking, if I hate it so much then maybe it is time for a change. But then to what? Unlike in 2023, today I have so many options - Bolero neo, Scorpio N, Seltos, etc., etc., to name a few. These are my standard go-to options, that have been around long enough (few more than others) that everybody else goes to too. And then there are EVs - new breed of cars taking over the road from their grand-old counterparts.

The recent geo-political developments have reshaped how we really think about EVs. Afterall if you switched to induction stoves, why not to EVs. Right?

Not necessarily. There are so many other factors at play here. It's not like you can simply plug into a socket and start driving - actually you can, just that it is not so straight forward. Isn't it? So that is what made me start thinking about EVs and what it would take to switch over to the other side, especially in India.

Let's be honest, we import almost 90% of all our crude oil to meet our domestic demands. Without going too much into the details, we already know how much of a headache importing crude oil has been over the years and especially recently due to the geopolitical developments.

And then there's the environmental impact - if we are the second largest importer, we might also be the burning so much - obviously, right? We've seen this every year during the winters for more than a decade. More and more of our cities get added to thsi list every year and this situation is also getting worse by the day.

Overall, crude oil has been on shaky grounds recently.

So, should we just cross-over to the other side?

The answer is NO. Because the other side is still, not only in India but even globally. So what's really the situation - are we there yet? Let's have a look.

## What would it take?

First and foremost - there is a huge shift in mindset that's required before moving to EVs. Today, my car buying decision is primarily based on my budget - Im a family man with two kids in a city like Bangalore - budget is always the deciding factor for all instance and purposes. I walk into a Maruti showroom and pick the car that best suits my budget and spend a little more here and there to "add-on" whatever comfort add-ons I want.

Buying an EV is a little more complex than that.

 a. Range -  First comes the usage - I drive 40kms daily during weekdays. Then there are trips to my home town - a mere 380 kms away - during weekends and holidays. And then there are occasionaly really long trips like Goa, Murdeswar, etc., once a year. Do you see where I am going with this? I can't just walk in to a showroom and buy what my budget allows but I have to buy what gives me the 'range' I need to cover these distances without any major hiccups. For example - my budget could allow a MG Comet, but that does not mean I buy that. I need something that has a longer range - something that has more than 55kWh battery to keep up with my need.

B. Charge -
Now, with a petrol or diesel car, I can easily do the trips I mentioned earlier, with refilling stations available almost everywhere on every highway nowadays. But with an EV, it needs a lot of planning as you can imagine. And I dare not plan it wrong, as it would mean that I will never hear the end of it from my family - especially considering some charging can take more than a few hours to top-up.

And not to forget the types of charging opions available. Connect to the wrong one and your life could momentarily turn hellish. Apparently, there is more than one type or one mode to charge your EV. And it is so complicated that it really deserves it own article - trust me I tried to place it here, but I rewrote it so many times that I finally gave up. From what I've read and understood so far, and to keep things tidy here - you only need to know 2 types - Type 2 and SSC2. These are the most commonly used types of - call it charger or ports - but this is all you need. The former connects your EV to AC (home/ office points) - slow charging and the later provides fast charging - anywhere between 25 mins to a couple of hours depending on charger speed/ capacity and what your car supports. I will put up a separate blog on this to cover the details.

So does that budget matter at all? Yes, it does.

C. Budget - 
You usage decides your battery capacity and hence the range you need your on your EV. This inturn controls the cost of the car. The higher the range, the higher the cost. Not exactly apple to apple when you compare with Petrol or Diesel, but close enough. The simple way I look at it - let's say you have a Petrol or Diesel car that costed ₹10 Lakhs on-road. You took it out the showroom and replaced the drivetrain to electric - remember this is not a technology supported today, only using as an example, so please don't go around quoting me anywhere.

Alright, so lets say you swap your petrol or diesel internal combustion engines (ICE) with an electric motor and then add battery to fuel this motor. The battery alone can add upa considerable cost of upto 25% of the total value. And then there is a redesign involved to accomodate - weight distribution, Thermal and Battery Management, and the software to connect and control all of these changes. These involve a considerable amount of re-engineering, that adds to the cost. And that roughly explains the price gap between our regular ICE (petrol/ diesel) based cars and EVs. The battery alone contributes to 25% and in some cases upto 40% of the cost of the vehicle.

So what does all this mean to you? Simply put - it means that EVs are pretty darn costly. Right?

Almost, if you only consider the upfront cost. And what I mean by that is - even though you pay a scary amount of money to purchase a vehicle, your running cost is almost negligible. Its like buying a Ferrari or a Lambo and driving it cost free. Really.

## The Math (This Is Where It Gets Interesting)

Let's look at the math first and trust me, this is where it really gets exciting. This is the - "You'll see God" moment.

Like I said I own a petrol car (which I hate) and to avoid any litigations I refrain from naming the brand or the model. Lets just say its a small seven seater car that gives me 10kmpl average in the city. And this is the real average that any other car will give you in Bangalore's traffic, if not lesser - don't fall for your friend's tall claims - "It give me 22kms even in traffic". I've heard a lot of such claims and they probably ride a bike.

So a petrol car gives you 10Kms average in city and maybe a little higher on highways - average it at 12kms.

I drive almost 1250 kms per month - daily 40 on city roads, occassionally on highways when heading to my hometown or longer trips.

So, that converts very roughly upto ₹11/km. Really, that's what we all spend everyday to drive. It probably is a little cheaper with diesel (consider ₹95 per liter), but it 'still' is damn costly per km.

Anyways, when you compare the cost per kilometer for an EV, its between ₹1 - ₹4 per km.

Need I say more?

## Here's my take on this topic so far -

Yes, EVs are costly to buy. But they are cheaper to run and maintain.
Yes, petrol and diesel driven cars have been around for a long time, while EVs are brand new in markets like India. But its not so far behind.
Yes, EV infrastructure is still playing catchup, but at a tremendous speed.

And, it looks like EVs are here to stay and maybe I should start considering this segment more seriously, when researching for new cars for myself and my family.

And if I am unable to afford one now (and it could be true too), it is probably time to consider it as a worthy contender to learn and research more about it. So stay with me, by following me here and together we can explore, learn and eventually buy ourselves that perfect car.
